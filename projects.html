The Challenge

"We look like every other growth equity firm. We don't act like any of them."

Vanta Capital manages £2.4bn in AUM and holds a genuinely differentiated thesis: they back infrastructure-layer technology businesses that most growth investors don't have the technical fluency to understand. The problem was that their brand — a navy wordmark and a PDF deck — communicated nothing of that sophistication.

They were being measured against their presentation, not their thinking. The best founders they wanted to back were going elsewhere because the brand signalled a conventional firm. We needed to build something that communicated intellectual authority before a single conversation had taken place.

We began with brand strategy: auditing every competitor in the UK growth equity space and mapping the visual language they shared (dark blue, serif wordmarks, handshake stock photography). Then we deliberately built in the opposite direction. The Vanta mark is a geometric abstraction — the intersection of a radial system and a right-angle vector — representing the structural precision they bring to complex deals. The palette is near-black with a single gold accent. The website is built for founders, not LPs: long-form thinking, published thesis documents, and an editorial voice that treats the reader as an equal.

The Challenge

"Every AI company looks clinical or futuristic. We want to look like something that actually understands humans."

Neura Labs had built a research platform that let non-technical teams interact with complex machine learning models through natural language — extraordinary capability housed in a forgettable product. Their previous design language communicated "startup trying to look like a serious enterprise." They needed to communicate what the product actually felt like to use: fluid, intelligent, intuitive.

We designed the brand around the concept of a thinking surface — the quiet, patient texture of intelligence at work. The visual language draws from node architecture (the connective tissue of neural networks) and translates it into something organic and accessible. Deep indigo grounds the palette; warm amber acts as the signal colour, the one thing worth paying attention to.

The website itself is an AI experience — the hero section responds to natural language queries in real time, demonstrating the product before a single feature page is visited. The interaction was designed to feel conversational rather than demonstrative; less product tour, more dialogue. Every animation follows an ease-out principle that mirrors natural human attention.

The Challenge

"The wellness market is full of brands that look like they've been designed by the same algorithm. We have a genuine story. Help us tell it."

Flora Botanicals sources medicinal plants from small-scale farmers across Southern Africa and formulates them into skincare and supplement products with genuine clinical evidence behind them. Their problem: they looked indistinguishable from the greenwashed, aspirational aesthetic that dominates the wellness category. Oat milk beige. Soft sans-serifs. The same stock photography of women staring pensively at mountains.

We rebuilt the brand around the concept of botanical exactness — the precision of a field botanist's drawing, the specificity of a plant that has been studied and understood, not romanticised. The visual system uses archival botanical illustration translated into motion: each product line is anchored by a hand-drawn plant form that comes to life across packaging, digital, and retail. The palette is drawn directly from the plants themselves — the deep green of Buchu, the amber of Honeybush, the pale sage of Rooibos leaf.

The launch campaign, "Every Leaf Has a Name," ran across digital, OOH, and in-store. Each execution featured a single plant, named and located, with copy that explained precisely what it did and why it was there. In a category built on vague wellness language, specificity was the disruptive act.
